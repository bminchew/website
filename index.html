Brent Minchew

Professor of Geophysics
Division of Geological and Planetary Sciences
California Institute of Technology
I study how glaciers and ice sheets respond to climate change, with emphasis on the mechanisms that will govern rates of sea-level rise in my daughter's lifetime. My research spans scales from atomic to continental using satellite radar, mathematical theory, and machine learning to study the mechanics of glacier beds, ice rheology, ice-ocean interactions, and fracture in glacier ice. My work allows me to see nature at its most elegant, both fragile and resilient, stately and responsive.

The Group

We are an interdisciplinary group of geophysicists, glaciologists, mechanicians, and geodesists who study the interactions between the climate, the cryosphere, and the solid earth. We use a combination of geodetic observations—primarily interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR)—and physical models to study dynamical systems and their various responses to environmental forcing.

We take a natural-laboratory approach to science and prefer to focus on observable short-timescale (hourly to sub-decadal) variations in the dynamics of large-scale systems in response to known forcings. Our ultimate goal is to establish clear connections between observations and first principles. Read more.

Research Highlights

Mechanics of Glacier Beds

Understanding how deformable beds modulate ice flow, using InSAR observations of ice caps and ice streams.

Ice Rheology & Shear Zones

Constraining the rheological properties of glacier ice in shear margins and their role in ice sheet stability.

Fracture & Calving

Mechanics of rift propagation and calving in ice shelves and their implications for ice sheet mass loss.

Ice-Ocean Interactions

Grounding zone dynamics and the response of glaciers to ocean forcing at tidal to decadal timescales.
