Research

We seek to understand connections between various components of the Earth system, their response to climate change, and human impact on the environment. We emphasize basic, curiosity-driven science and prefer to study physical processes in the natural environment through observations and a hierarchical modeling approach.

Most of our efforts focus on glacier and ice sheet dynamics. This work typically relies on remote-sensing observations of ice velocities and fundamental mechanics, organized as three broad, interconnected research topics: (1) the mechanics of deformable glacier beds, (2) the rheology of ice in shear zones, and (3) the mechanics of calving and rifting. Each of these serves to improve projections of future sea-level rise and to bolster our broader understanding of ice sheet evolution.

Occasionally, we develop remote sensing tools and applications that can help mitigate natural and anthropogenic environmental hazards, including methods for characterizing marine oil spills and monitoring wildfires.

Mechanics of Deformable Glacier Beds

The flow of glaciers and ice sheets is strongly influenced by conditions at the ice-bed interface. We use airborne and spaceborne InSAR observations of temperate ice caps in Iceland and ice streams in West Antarctica to constrain the rheological properties of subglacial till and the mechanics of basal sliding. This work combines geodetic observations with numerical ice-flow models and laboratory-derived constitutive laws to study the coupling between ice flow and bed deformation.

Ice Rheology & Shear Margins

Shear margins are narrow zones of intense deformation that bound fast-flowing ice streams. The rheological properties of ice in these regions control the lateral resistance to flow and are critical for ice sheet stability. We use high-resolution velocity observations and inverse methods to constrain the effective viscosity of glacier ice and study how damage and recrystallization affect ice rheology.

Fracture, Calving, & Rifting

The calving of icebergs from ice shelves is a primary mechanism of mass loss from ice sheets. We study how rifts initiate and propagate through ice shelves using time-series analysis of InSAR-derived strain-rate fields. This work aims to improve our understanding of the mechanical processes that lead to ice shelf disintegration and the subsequent acceleration of grounded ice.

Ice-Ocean Interactions

Grounding zones—where grounded ice begins to float—are among the most dynamic and consequential regions of ice sheets. We study how ocean forcing modulates ice flow at tidal to decadal timescales using geodetic observations and physical models, with emphasis on processes that govern rates of grounding-line retreat and ice sheet mass loss.

Kinematic Remote Sensing

We develop and apply methods for measuring surface velocities and deformation of glaciers using interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR), pixel/feature tracking, and multi-temporal analysis. These observations form the foundation for most of our research on glacier dynamics.

Hazard Response & Mitigation

We develop remote sensing tools for environmental hazard response, including methods for detecting and characterizing marine oil spills using polarimetric SAR and monitoring the extent and severity of wildfires. This work makes direct societal contributions and sharpens our remote sensing capabilities.
